# Data-Driven Design

Live site: https://tedngai.github.io/datadrivendesign/

Data-Driven Design is a public tutorial library for students and designers learning how to work with GIS, remote sensing, data visualization, and natural language processing in creative and research-based practice.

These tutorials were developed for design students who may be new to spatial analysis, coding, or data-driven workflows. The goal is to make technical tools approachable without flattening their complexity. Instead of treating GIS or data science as separate specialist domains, the tutorials frame them as practical methods for asking better design questions, testing claims with evidence, and communicating findings clearly.

## Who These Tutorials Are For

- Undergraduate and graduate design students
- Architects, landscape architects, urban designers, and planners
- Researchers who want a more accessible entry into GIS and data workflows
- Instructors looking for project-based technical teaching material

## What You Will Learn

- How to find, clean, and interpret spatial and non-spatial datasets
- How to use GIS and remote sensing tools to study climate, land, infrastructure, and urban change
- How to work with terrain, LiDAR, satellite imagery, and classified raster data
- How to build data visualizations and interactive outputs from real datasets
- How to approach technical workflows critically by thinking about scale, uncertainty, bias, and representation

## Topic Areas

### GIS and Remote Sensing

The GIS tutorials introduce core workflows such as terrain modeling, land cover analysis, multispectral imagery, LiDAR processing, sea level rise visualization, solar potential, and urban heat island mapping. These lessons connect spatial analysis to design questions about climate resilience, environmental justice, representation, and site understanding.

### Data Visualization and Data Science

The data tutorials focus on collecting, cleaning, analyzing, and presenting information in ways that support interpretation and storytelling. They include web scraping, exploratory data analysis, and visualization workflows using real cultural and institutional datasets.

### Natural Language Processing

The NLP tutorials introduce text-based analysis for design research, helping students understand how language data can be cleaned, explored, and interpreted as part of a broader inquiry process.

## Approach

These tutorials are written to be both practical and reflective. They explain how to complete a workflow, but they also ask what the data means, where it comes from, what it leaves out, and how design decisions change when evidence becomes visible.

The intended outcome is not just software literacy. It is a stronger ability to move from intuition to analysis, and from analysis to more grounded design proposals.
